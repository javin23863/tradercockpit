## 01 title-card
It's Saturday, and the week is in the books. The week that just closed handed you a pair of very different stories at the same time. Brent crude ripped more than seventeen percent on a shooting war around the Strait of Hormuz. And the Nasdaq one hundred dropped more than four percent in the same five sessions that the biggest banks in America printed record quarters. One of those stories says risk-off — sell everything, the cycle is turning. The other says rotation — money changing seats inside a bull market that is still standing. They cannot both be the whole truth, and your portfolio only cares which one wins. So this weekend review asks one question, and we're going to let the charts answer it piece by piece: did the bull just crack, or did money just change seats? ## 02 q-numbers
Question one. What did the week actually do? Not the headlines, not the scariest intraday print — the closes. Friday to Friday, off the weekly bars. Because the index number you saw on your phone is hiding most of what actually happened underneath it.

## 04 q-breadth
Next question. Did the selling spread? Every correction in history starts in one corner of the market. A crack becomes a break the day the selling stops being selective. So — did it spread, or did it stay in its lane?

## 05 breadth
This is the chart most people never look at: equal-weight S and P versus cap-weight, R S P over S P Y, weekly. Every stock in the index counted once, divided by the index where the giants count most. When this line falls, a handful of mega-caps are carrying the tape and the average stock is along for the ride. When it rises, the average stock is doing the work. This week — in a down tape — the ratio rose more than one percent. Sit with that for a moment, because it is the single most important fact of the week. The index fell one and a half percent, and the average stock inside it went UP relative to the index. Mechanically, that means the selling was not the whole index. It was concentrated at the very top of the cap table — the mega-cap tech complex, the exact handful of names that had carried the index for months and where every crowded dollar lived. Cross-check it with the small caps you just saw: the Russell gave up half a percent while the Nasdaq gave up four. Run the falsification test. If this were a genuine risk-off — a growth scare, a credit event, a liquidity problem — the pattern inverts: small caps lead the damage, equal-weight collapses against the index, and the generals fall last. That is what every real bear phase looks like at the start. The opposite printed this week. The soldiers advanced while the generals retreated. In the weight of the evidence, that is not what breaking looks like. That is what rotation looks like. But rotation has a destination. Where did the money actually go? That is the next question.

## 06 q-sectors
The next question. Did money play offense or defense? Rotation always leaves fingerprints — one sector's outflow is another sector's bid. Follow the fingerprints this week left on the tape.

## 07 sector-ratios
First fingerprint: energy. X L E, the energy sector fund, weekly. Fifty-five dollars to fifty-seven sixty-eight — up almost five percent in a week when the index fell. With Brent up seventeen, the producers confirmed the move, and confirmation is the word that matters. When crude spikes but the producers don't follow, the market is telling you it doesn't believe the price will stick. This week they followed with force: the war premium is flowing straight into the earnings of the companies that own the barrels, and money chased it. That is offense — aggressive, directional offense — in one specific sector. Second fingerprint: the consumer. Discretionary versus staples, X L Y over X L P, weekly. Down almost three percent. Money rotated from want-to-have toward need-to-have — from restaurants and retail toward toothpaste and groceries. That is the classic defensive fingerprint, the one that says portfolio managers trimmed risk without leaving the market. Third, and this is the chart of the week: semiconductors versus the S and P, S M H over S P X, weekly ratio. Down seven and a half percent. In one week. Chips did not just fall — they fell seven and a half percent faster than the market itself. And here is what makes that violent number readable: the news inside it was good. TSMC grew revenue thirty-six percent year over year and raised its capital spending plan toward sixty-four billion dollars — and the stocks got sold anyway. When good news gets sold, you are not watching fundamentals break. You are watching positioning unwind — crowded money leaving a crowded theater through a narrow door. The fourth fingerprint is the one that did not print, and it might be the loudest. High yield credit versus Treasuries, H Y G over I E F, weekly: down three tenths of one percent. Flat. Credit is where real trouble shows up earliest — junk bonds reprice hard and fast when the market smells default risk, and they do not wait for equities' permission. This week, while tech bled and missiles flew over the Gulf, credit did not move. So the four fingerprints read: offense in energy, defense in the consumer, a violent unwind in chips, and total calm at the exit doors. That is a market changing seats, sector by sector. It is not a market running for the exits.

## 08 q-trend
Now the trend question. Is the primary trend intact? A bad week inside an uptrend is noise — every bull market has dozens of them. A bad week that breaks the structure is signal. The weekly chart settles it, so let's look.

## 09 trend
Back to the S and P, weekly, and now read the structure instead of the week. The index closed at seventy-four fifty-eight — down on the week, but it held above the week's own low at seventy-four thirty-one, and the weekly uptrend that built through the spring is still standing underneath it. One down week of one and a half percent, after the run this index has had, does not break a weekly structure. It tests it. Tests are normal; failures are not. And the difference between them is one number: a weekly close below seventy-four thirty-one is where this stops being a test and starts being a change of character. Watch weekly closes, not Tuesday afternoons — intraday breaks that recover by Friday are how this market shakes out weak hands. Now the other trend on the board — the one that is accelerating. Brent crude, weekly. Seventy-five twenty-two to eighty-eight twenty-seven, a seventeen percent week, high at eighty-eight thirty-eight. And here is the part the headlines skip: the week before, oil was already up more than four and a half percent. The market was pricing Hormuz risk before Monday's strikes ever happened — this week poured fuel on a fire that was already lit. Two weekly bars, both up hard, both closing near their highs. That is trend, not spike, and trends in crude have a way of feeding themselves through hedging and short covering. The strikes came Monday, the blockade talk came with them — and then Tuesday the transit-fee proposal was withdrawn, with Bloomberg reporting flows easing along the waterway. Crude barely gave any of it back. When a market refuses to fall on de-escalation news, the premium is sticky. One more read rounds this out: the dollar index was flat on the week, down two tenths. No dollar squeeze, no liquidity event hiding under the surface — this is a clean head-to-head race. Equity trend bent but intact above seventy-four thirty-one. Oil trend up and accelerating through eighty-eight. Those two trends are now pulling on each other, and there is a referee. Which brings us to the bond market.

## 10 q-bonds
The last question. What does the bond market say? An oil shock is supposed to be an inflation shock, and an inflation shock is supposed to show up in yields within hours. The ten-year Treasury is where the fear has to appear. Did it?

## 11 divergences
Here is the ten-year yield, weekly. The week oil rose seventeen percent, the ten-year went from four point five six to four point five five. One basis point. LOWER. The single most inflation-sensitive price on the planet looked at the biggest oil week of the year and did not move. Why not? Because the same week delivered the other side of the ledger, almost hour for hour. Tuesday morning, June C P I printed three point five against three point eight expected — a cooler print, with core at two point six and core month-over-month at zero. Wednesday, producer prices actually FELL three tenths, with the energy pipeline components falling hard — gasoline down twelve percent inside that report. The bond market netted a shooting war against a cooling inflation trend and called it a wash. And the pricing tells you which way the room leans: Reuters reported markets putting only about a fourteen percent chance on a July hike, with roughly thirty basis points of hikes priced by December. That is a market debating whether policy needs to lean harder — not one bracing for an inflation spiral. Then get another opinion. Gold, weekly. With missiles in the air and a blockade on the front page, the oldest war hedge in the world fell about two and a half percent, closing near four thousand eighteen. CNBC called it the biggest weekly loss in six weeks. Read those together, because this is the spine of the whole review. No fear bid in the metal. No fear bid in bonds. No stress in credit. No squeeze in the dollar. Outside of the equity volatility complex, the deepest markets on earth looked at this week and refused to panic. Either every one of them is wrong at the same time — or the equity move was never really about the war at all.

## 12 q-verdict
So. The verdict question. Every answer is on the table. Weigh them together the way a portfolio manager has to: should you walk into Monday confident, or concerned? Here is the honest tally — both columns, nothing hidden.

## 13 weight-of-evidence
The concern column is real, so give it its due. Semiconductors lost seven and a half percent against the market in five sessions — unwinds like that can cascade if the crowd all runs at once. Technology, the largest sector in your index, fell five and a half. The consumer rotated defensive. The VIX rose twenty-five percent. And a seventeen percent oil week is an inflation risk with a lit fuse — if crude keeps climbing, the bond market's calm gets tested week by week. Now the confidence column. The average stock went UP against the index. Small caps barely fell. Credit did not move. Yields did not move. Gold went DOWN. And fear never closed above twenty. Weigh them honestly and a pattern jumps out. Everything in the concern column lives inside one complex — mega-cap tech and the A I infrastructure trade, where TSMC's own spending plans and a guidance stumble at Netflix handed crowded money its excuse to leave. Everything in the confidence column is the rest of the entire financial system — bonds, credit, gold, the dollar, two thousand small caps — declining to confirm the scare. The weight of the evidence, as of Friday's close, reads: a concentrated unwind inside the market's most crowded trade, funding an orderly rotation into energy and the average stock, all of it under a real, live oil-shock overlay that the bond market is so far refusing to price. That is not a broken bull. It is also not an all-clear. It is a market on watch — and the next few weekly closes decide which column grows. We do not predict which. We mark the levels, and we let the tape vote.
