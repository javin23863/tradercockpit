## 07 how the risk reaches markets
The market mechanism has a few steps, and each one can fail. First, danger around a major shipping route raises the cost and uncertainty of moving cargo. That can mean insurance, freight, delays, rerouting, and compliance checks. Second, those costs can raise the delivered price of energy and products even before a refinery loses supply. Third, higher delivered-energy risk makes the inflation and rate path less comfortable. That is where energy stocks can hold up while long-duration growth gets marked down.

This is why the IMO register matters more than a viral clip, and why the Treasury notices matter differently from the shipping record. The IMO can confirm vessel incidents. Treasury can change who banks and operators are allowed to deal with. Neither one, by itself, tells us exactly how many barrels moved through Hormuz today. I am keeping those claims separate.

For portfolios, the exposed side is clear. Energy producers benefit if the premium stays in crude. Vessel owners, cargo interests, refiners, airlines, and other fuel-intensive businesses carry the operating risk. Rate-sensitive growth stays under pressure if yields remain firm. I do not have a same-day chart for every one of those businesses, so I am not assigning them a move they did not print in this dashboard.
