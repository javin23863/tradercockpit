## 01 hook-oil
If you own stocks, energy, or gold, this is the week the Strait of Hormuz starts showing up in your portfolio. Over the weekend a tanker was hit inside the strait and Iran declared it closed. On Monday, the President announced a naval blockade of Iran, and a toll on every ship that passes. Oil jumped about four percent. Energy was the best sector in the market. Chip stocks got hit. In the next few minutes: what actually happened, the exact levels I am watching on oil, the S and P, and gold, and the two things happening before today's open that can move your account.

## 02 news-blockade
Here is the headline that moved the money. On Monday, President Trump announced America is reinstating its blockade of Iran, and declared the United States the guardian of the strait. The part that hits costs directly: a toll of twenty percent of cargo value for safe passage through Hormuz, on a waterway that carries about a fifth of the world's oil. Iran's military command answered within hours, warning of a forceful response to any American attempt to manage the strait. Why you should care: every dollar of added shipping cost shows up in the price of crude, and crude is an input cost for almost everything else you own.

## 03 news-strikes
Now the war itself, and keep fact separate from claim, because markets trade on both. The American account: Central Command says it struck roughly three hundred targets over three nights, about a hundred and forty in the latest wave. Iran's account: its Revolutionary Guard claims strikes on Bahrain and Oman, including a radar site. That is not independently confirmed. What maritime authorities did confirm: another tanker was hit near the Omani coast on Monday, structural damage and a fire. Markets do not wait to find out who is telling the truth. They price the risk immediately, and that risk premium is now sitting in every barrel of crude.

## 04 news-transits
So is the strait actually closed? For your money, that is the wrong question. The right question is what it costs to move oil through it now. Independent trackers counted about fourteen ships transiting on Monday, half of them Iranian. Normal traffic runs around a hundred and thirty a day. The US military claims more than a hundred and forty ships made it through in the last seven days. Pick either number, the direction is the same: traffic has collapsed. And war-risk insurance now runs between two and six percent of a ship's value, adding millions of dollars per voyage. That cost lands in the crude price. The crude price lands in earnings. Earnings land in your portfolio.

## 05 oil-ta
Let's put levels on it. This is Brent, daily chart. Friday's close was seventy-six fifty-six. That line is now support. Monday ripped through it to around seventy-nine, up about four percent on the day, the highest price since late June. Above us, the level this whole story trades against: back in March, when insurers pulled war coverage from the strait, Brent printed one twenty-six. That is the panic high. As long as headlines keep coming out of the Gulf, dips toward Friday's close are getting bought. If diplomacy sticks, that support breaks, and the war premium comes out of this chart fast.

## 06 spx-ta
Now the index your retirement money actually tracks. The S and P five hundred closed Monday at seven thousand five hundred forty-seven, down about half a percent. That is not panic. That is a market politely stepping back. The Nasdaq fell close to one percent, dragged by chips. Here is the level that matters: Friday's close, seventy-five seventy-five. Across a full weekend of war, the index barely moved, which tells you stocks are treating this as an oil story, not a growth story. That read changes the day the S and P starts losing Friday's level with conviction. Watch that line before you touch anything in your account.

## 07 xle-rotation
Under a flat index, the real trade. Energy was the best performing sector in the market on Monday. The energy fund, ticker X L E, closed up about two and a half percent, because higher crude flows straight into producer earnings. The other side of the rotation: chips. Nvidia, A M D, and Intel each fell more than three percent, and the tech sector dragged. This is the classic war-premium rotation. Money leaves growth and crowds into the barrel. If you hold tech-heavy funds, this is exactly why your account was red on a day the index barely moved. If Hormuz stays hot, this is the trade the market keeps repeating.

## 08 gold-ta
Gold. The hedge everyone buys for war, and it is not doing its job. Gold peaked near five thousand five hundred ninety-five back in January, before the worst of this fighting, and it has slid to around four thousand one hundred, down more than ten percent from the high. On Monday, with missiles in the air, it fell again. The reason is on the bond side: a ten-year Treasury yield around four and a half percent and a firm dollar pay you to wait in cash and bonds instead of metal. If gold is sitting in your portfolio as war insurance, understand that this year the war premium went to oil, not to gold.

## 09 why-hormuz
Step back. Why does one waterway set the price of everything? Scale. About twenty million barrels of oil move through Hormuz every day. That is a fifth of everything the world consumes, and more than a quarter of all the oil that travels by sea. Add roughly a fifth of the world's liquefied natural gas, including ninety-three percent of Qatar's exports. There is no pipeline network that replaces it. That is why one tanker strike moves your energy stocks, your airline costs, and the inflation number the Federal Reserve is staring at.

## 10 news-supply
Now the twist the war headlines bury: the physical market is not short of oil. On Monday, OPEC cut its demand growth forecast for this year, citing a weaker China. And the International Energy Agency says global supply jumped by more than four million barrels a day in June, even though world output still sits well below pre-war levels. Translation for your positions: today's price is fear, not shortage. Fear premiums deflate fast on good news. If the weekend talks produce anything real, the air comes out of this trade quickly, and it will not send you a warning.

## 11 scenarios
So where can crude actually go? These are scenarios, not predictions, and they matter because they are what the big desks are positioned around. Goldman Sachs laid out a case where, if the strait stays mostly shut another month, Brent averages above a hundred dollars for the rest of the year, with a third quarter path toward a hundred and twenty. JPMorgan flagged a spike zone of a hundred and twenty to a hundred and thirty, with overshoot toward a hundred and fifty if disruption persists. Government models of a one-quarter halt in Gulf exports put crude near a hundred and ten. Every one of those lines is on this chart. None of them is a forecast. They are the map of what the market fears.
