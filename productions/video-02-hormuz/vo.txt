## 01 approach-coldopen

Here is what happened while you were away, and what it does to your open this morning.

## 02 strait-flyover
This is the strait. At its tightest it is twenty-one nautical miles across, about thirty-nine kilometers, with Iran on one shore and Oman on the other. Saturday night the Cyprus-flagged container ship GFS Galaxy was hit right here: heavy engine damage, one of twelve crew missing. Iran's Revolutionary Guard declared the strait closed. Washington answered overnight. Central Command counts roughly three hundred targets over three nights, a hundred and forty in the latest wave alone. Then on Sunday, Iran hit back, striking bases in Oman, Qatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, and Jordan.

## 03 gate-orbit
So is the strait actually closed? It depends who you ask. Iran says yes. The American president went on Sunday television and said it is open. The naval coordination center splits the difference: a southern lane, hugging the Omani coast, is still moving traffic both ways. The tracking data tells the real story. Twenty-one ships transited on July eleventh. Before the war, this waterway averaged about a hundred and forty a day. And war-risk insurance for a single supertanker run now costs roughly eight times what it did before the crisis. A strait does not have to be shut to choke. It only has to get too expensive to cross.

## 05 why-hormuz
Why does one waterway move the entire market? The numbers. About twenty million barrels of oil a day move through Hormuz. That is roughly a fifth of everything the world burns, and more than a quarter of all the oil that travels by sea. Add about a fifth of the planet's liquefied natural gas, including ninety-three percent of Qatar's exports. There is no pipeline, no railway, and no detour that replaces it. When this strait tightens, the oil has nowhere else to go.

## 07 sectors
We already have a preview of how this trades. Wednesday the eighth, when the ceasefire first fell apart. If the weekend rhymes with Wednesday, you can already guess which side of it your portfolio is sitting on.

## 08 gold-dollar
And then there is gold, refusing to do its job. The playbook says war sends gold higher. Instead, gold set a record near fifty-five ninety-five back in January, before the worst of this fighting, and it has since slid to around four thousand one hundred. Down about thirteen percent. A stronger dollar and a ten-year Treasury yield above four and a half percent have simply outmuscled the fear trade. If you bought gold as war insurance this year, it has not paid out.
