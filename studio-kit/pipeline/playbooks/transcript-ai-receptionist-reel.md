# Transcript playbook — AI receptionist reel

# CRAFT EXTRACTION

## 1. FORMAT & PACING
- **Type:** Short-form direct-response video (Reels/TikTok/Shorts) — a pitch-with-proof, not a tutorial or vlog.
- **Rhythm:** ~90 words, 9 sentences → roughly 30–35s spoken. Average sentence ≈ 10 words; one idea per sentence, zero throat-clearing, no greeting, nothing restated.
- **Cut logic (implied by script density):** every sentence is a discrete beat → supports a cut or visual change every ~3 seconds. Transcript-only caveat: actual cut timing, visuals, and platform aren't observable here — pacing judgments are inferred from script structure, not footage.

## 2. HOOK
**Technique:** Three-layer stack in the first ~5 seconds — *command → audience filter → loss-framed stakes*.
- Layer 1 (pattern interrupt): direct imperative aimed at the scrolling behavior itself.
- Layer 2 (qualification): names the viewer by *behavior*, not demographic — instant self-selection.
- Layer 3 (stakes): names a loss, not a benefit, and makes it ongoing ("every single day").

**QUOTE (opening):**
> "Stop scrolling. If you run a local business and you're still answering your own phone, you're losing money every single day."

## 3. STRUCTURE (beat-by-beat)
1. **Hard stop** — "Stop scrolling." (reclaims attention)
2. **Audience filter** — "If you run a local business…" (self-selection by behavior)
3. **Pain/stakes** — "you're losing money every single day." (loss framing)
4. **Promise of explanation** — "Here's why:" (mini open loop)
5. **Stat-as-problem** — "the average business misses 30% of its calls. That's 30% of new customers, gone." (restates the number in the unit that hurts)
6. **Pivot** — "But what if…" (solution turn — notably the only question in the script)
7. **Mechanism (rule of three)** — "answered every call, booked the appointment, and texted the customer back in seconds" (three concrete verbs, zero adjectives)
8. **Proof unit** — "I built one for a plumber in Detroit last week. He booked 12 extra jobs in 5 days." (first-person authority + niche + city + count + timeframe)
9. **Stakes callback** — "That's real money from calls he used to miss." (closes the loop opened in the hook)
10. **CTA** — "Comment 'AI' and I'll send you exactly how it works." (one-word trigger, defined payoff, the only ask)

## 4. RETENTION TACTICS
- **Open loop:** "Here's why:" promises a reason and delays it one beat.
- **"But" pivot:** "But what if…" reverses direction mid-video — the classic but/so momentum engine — and switches the script's only interrogative sentence on at the exact moment attention needs reopening.
- **Reframe for impact:** doesn't stop at "misses 30% of calls" — immediately translates to "30% of new customers, gone." Same number, higher emotional weight.
- **Number escalation:** abstract percentage (30%) → concrete outcome count (12 jobs) → money ("real money"). Each successive number is more tangible than the last.
- **Specificity as credibility:** "plumber in Detroit," "last week," "12 extra jobs," "5 days," "in seconds" — granular anchors that make the claim hard to dismiss as fabricated.
- **Return to opening:** ends on "calls he used to miss," mirroring the hook's pain — the arc feels resolved, so the CTA lands on a closed loop rather than interrupting an open one.

## 5. SCRIPTING PATTERNS
- **Imperative first word.** Opens with a command, not a question or greeting.
- **Loss frame before gain frame.** Pain ("losing money") precedes any mention of the solution.
- **Rule of three for mechanism.** Exactly three actions ("answered… booked… texted") — feels complete and memorizable.
- **Quantify everything.** 30%, 12 jobs, 5 days, "seconds." No "a lot," no "many."
- **"That's ___" reframe move.** State a stat, then immediately restate it in the unit the viewer actually cares about (customers, money). Used twice.
- **Present-tense problem, past-tense proof.** The problem is happening *now*; the fix already worked *once* — "I built one… last week" doubles as an authority claim (the creator is the operator, not a commentator).
- **One sentence = one job.** No compound run-ons; every line advances exactly one beat.
- **Only one question, at the pivot.** Declaratives carry pain and proof; the single "what if" carries the turn.

## 6. CTA STYLE
- **Lowest-friction action:** comment a single word ("AI") — not "link in bio," not "follow," not "DM me."
- **Trigger word is short and topical** — easy to type, easy to auto-reply to.
- **Defined deliverable:** "I'll send you *exactly how it works*" — the viewer knows precisely what they get, unlike a vague "follow for more."
- **Single ask, no dilution:** there is exactly one CTA — no stacked "like, follow, and share" competing for the click.
- **Placement:** immediately after the proof beat, while the "12 extra jobs" high is still active.

## 7. TRANSFERABLE RULES
1. **When opening, don't greet — command or confront**, because greetings earn swipes while a directive ("Stop scrolling") or bold claim earns the next beat.
2. **When filtering the audience, name a behavior, not a demographic** ("still answering your own phone" > "local business owners"), because a symptom lets viewers self-diagnose in one second.
3. **When stating a stat, immediately restate it in the unit of pain or gain the viewer feels** ("30% of calls → 30% of new customers, gone"), because the translation *is* the persuasion.
4. **When introducing the solution, pivot with "But" / "What if,"** because the reversal reopens attention exactly where mid-video drop-off happens — and reserving your only question for this beat sharpens the turn.
5. **When describing what a product does, list exactly three concrete actions, never adjectives** ("answered, booked, texted" > "smart, fast, reliable"), because verbs are verifiable and adjectives are noise.
6. **When giving proof, stack four specifics — niche, location, count, timeframe — and tell it first-person past tense** ("I built one for a plumber in Detroit… 12 jobs in 5 days"), because granularity reads as real and "I built" embeds authority inside the proof.
7. **When escalating numbers, move abstract → concrete → money** (percentage → job count → "real money"), because each step makes the stakes more tangible.
8. **When closing the value case, call back to the opening pain** ("calls he used to miss"), because symmetry makes the arc feel finished and the solution feel earned — then place the CTA on that closed loop.
9. **When asking for action, make it one word, on-platform, with a named deliverable, and make it the only ask,** because friction kills conversion, ambiguity kills trust, and stacked CTAs split the click.
10. **When scripting 30–40s, run the minimal complete arc — Command → Filter → Pain → Stat-reframe → Pivot → Mechanism ×3 → Proof → Callback → CTA —** and cut any sentence that doesn't add pain, proof, mechanism, or payoff, because at this length every beat must be load-bearing.
